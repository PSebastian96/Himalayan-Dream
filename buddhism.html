Buddhism

About Buddhism

Buddhism is a non-theistic system. That is to say, they do not believe in the existence of a supreme being. However, it is a way of life. And, Buddhism is beyond religion. In addition, about an estimated 488 million in the world practice Buddhism. Hence, it represents up to 9% to 10% of the world’s total population.

Buddhism as a philosophy exists across many cultures and periods. Also, it goes with variable teachings and practices. Despite the differences among Buddhist traditions, they still share a standard set of core beliefs.

Thus, in Buddhism, the primary purpose of life is to end suffering. The Buddha teachings emphasize that humans suffer because of earthly pleasures. In the same manner, he taught that these pleasures mask the rate of human suffering. Therefore, Buddhism teaches us the importance of recognizing the impermanence of all things. And, above all, how to free oneself from such attachments.

The Four Noble Truths:

The First Noble Truth

Life is made of suffering (Dukkha). Suffering can come in many forms, including old age, sickness, and death. These are the first sights the Buddha saw outside his palace.

The Second Noble Truth

The causes of suffering are desire and attachment. Root causes include thirst, failure, and pain, deeply rooted in desire (tanhā).

The Third Noble Truth

Suffering can be stopped (Nirodha) by liberating oneself from attachment and extinguishing greed, delusion, and hatred.

The Fourth Noble Truth

The way to end suffering is to follow the Noble Eightfold Path, a set of principles for liberation.

The Five Precepts and The Eightfold Path:

The Five Precepts

The Five Precepts are ethical guidelines for Buddhists, undertaken voluntarily:

  • Abstinence from Murder
  • Abstinence from Alcoholism
  • Sexual Misconduct
  • Abstinence from Stealing
  • Refrain from Evil-Minded Thoughts

The Eightfold Path

A path of Buddhist practices leading to liberation from samsara and nirvana:

  • Right View/Understanding
  • Right Thinking
  • Right Speech
  • Right Conduct
  • Right Livelihood
  • Right Effort
  • Right Mindfulness
  • Right Concentration

Buddhist Beliefs:

Meditation

Meditation focuses the mind on achieving inner stillness and enlightenment. It helps attain awareness, reduces worries, and slows brain aging.

Karma

Karma reflects the consequences of actions. Positive or negative deeds influence life roles, character development, and resource use.

  • - How we see our role in life
  • - How we treat others
  • - Character development
  • - Resource use

Reincarnation

Reincarnation is rebirth after death. In Buddhism, it may not always mean returning as the same entity but emphasizes liberation from the cycle.
